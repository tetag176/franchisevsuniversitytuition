I walked the digital streets of New York — through Google Maps, tuition pages, and café counts — to see how Starbucks vs independent coffee shops cluster around ten NYC universities.

What I measured

  • Annual undergraduate tuition
  • # of Starbucks within ~10-minute walk
  • # of independent cafés
  • Share of cafés that are Starbucks vs indie
  • Neighborhood type (arts, business, residential…)

Hypothesis

I started with a simple guess:
“Universities with higher tuition have more Starbucks and fewer independent cafés within a 10-minute walk of campus.”

In my head, expensive schools felt like they would attract more corporate brands, while cheaper or more community-focused campuses would be surrounded by smaller, local cafés.

So I picked 10 NYC universities, pulled tuition from official websites, then used Google Maps to count every Starbucks vs independent café within walking distance.

Does tuition really mean more Starbucks?

First, the whole picture

Each dot is a university. The further right, the higher the tuition. The higher up, the larger the share of nearby cafés that are Starbucks. I expected the dots to climb up as they moved right.

FIT: cheaper tuition, super corporate coffee

FIT sits in a fashion / commercial zone in Chelsea. Tuition is relatively low for NYC, but about 68% of nearby cafés are Starbucks. It’s flooded with chains — fast coffee for busy shoppers and workers.

Baruch: Midtown business district = Starbucks central

Baruch is in a corporate part of Midtown. Its tuition is low compared to private schools, but 70% of cafés in walking distance are Starbucks. The neighborhood is more “9–5 office” than “slow indie espresso bar.”

The New School: pricey, but indie-heavy

Around The New School, Starbucks only makes up about 33% of the café scene. The rest are independent spots. The area feels more like a design / arts neighborhood, and the coffee options reflect that.

Pratt: arts campus, indie cafés everywhere

Pratt Institute in Brooklyn has a high tuition and an arts-focused identity. But just 16.7% of cafés nearby are Starbucks — the rest are local, independent cafés, which line up with a slower, creative neighborhood vibe.

Fordham (Bronx): residential, community coffee

Fordham also has higher tuition, but it’s in a more residential, neighborhood-based part of the Bronx. Only 25% of nearby cafés are Starbucks — most of the coffee is local.

Neighborhood colors tell a second story

The dot colors group campuses by neighborhood type. Business and corporate areas tend to float higher on the chart — more Starbucks. Artsy, local, or residential neighborhoods usually sit lower, with more indie cafés in the mix.

So… tuition wasn’t the full story

Instead of “higher tuition = more Starbucks,” the pattern leans the other way: some of the cheaper schools in business districts have the most Starbucks, while expensive, artsy schools are surrounded by independent coffee shops.

What I learned

My hypothesis was wrong in a useful way. Higher tuition did not automatically mean more Starbucks. Instead, the café landscape around each campus is shaped more by neighborhood identity than tuition alone.

Lower-tuition schools in dense business districts (like FIT and Baruch) ended up with the most Starbucks, while higher-tuition, arts-oriented or residential campuses (like The New School, Pratt, and Fordham) were surrounded by independent cafés. Coffee isn’t just about caffeine here—it’s a map of how corporate convenience and local culture balance in different slices of New York City.

In other words: show me your campus coffee, and I can guess the neighborhood you live in.
